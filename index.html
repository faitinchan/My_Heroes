The Most Influential Figures in My Life

The following are the figures I look up to, including historical, fictional, mythical, and comtemporary characters. I've listed them in chronological order according to their first appearance in my life. Each of them has significantly shaped who I am today and represents different stages of my journey to adulthood.

Jesus

After attending kindergarten, my mother enrolled me in a Christian primary school, where the teachers were all Christians. Thanks to their patience and support, they helped mold me from a restless, naughty kid who often disrupted the class into someone who could at least remain seated. However, the more direct influence of Jesus Christ on me began when I was 11. A family member took me to a church to attend a small gospel meeting. The narrative of heaven and hell led me to repent for my past wrongdoings. Since then, I dedicated a significant amount of time and energy to understanding and following the Bible until about two years ago.

Albert Einstein

The summer before I entered secondary school, I read a biography of Einstein. Unlike other biographies, his journey into discovering the theory of relativity fascinated me. The theory itself was captivating, and his story inspired me to dream of becoming a theoretical physicist, setting me on a path in the STEM field. However, the romanticized portrayal of him in the biography also led me to imitate the stereotypical "crazy scientist" image, focusing on intense, innovative thinking at the expense of mundane daily life.

Yang Guo

Yang Guo, courtesy name Gaizhi, is the fictional protagonist of Jin Yong's wuxia novel, The Return of the Condor Heroes. After discovering my oficial IQ test score, measured by a psychiatrist, I identified with Yang Guo due to the similarities in our intelligence and personality traits. I deeply resonated with his challenges and internal conflicts, often drawing parallels with my own situation. This led to a typical "me against the world" mentality, which presisted into my late adolescence.

Moritaka Mashiro

During high school, I became obsessed with Japanese manga. Moritaka Mashiro, the main character in Tsugumi Ohba and Takeshi Obata's manga series Bakuman (the same creators behind Death Note), inspired me. The story and the characters' dedication to their craft captivated me, and I even bought drawing tools to create my own manga. I experimented with my style, drawing inspiration from Salvador Dalí and M.C. Escher. However, my passion for manga came at a cost, and my academic performance suffered, a trend that persisted until I graduated from university.

Marshall Bruce Mathers III

I discovered Eminem's song Without Me through the moive Suicide Squad. The satirical vibe resonated with me, particularly his critique of societal norms, which aligned with my own frustrations with the church I was part of at the time. I delved deeper into his music and the hip-hop genre, finding the rhymes Eminem, Nas, and Tupac to be especially impactful. Their music became a source of solace during my darkest times, motivating me to face challenges head-on. However, my admiration for them also led to some imitation, influencing my style, speech, and behavior.

Jordan B. Peterson

As I grew increasingly frustrated with the church, I sought alternative perspectives on the Bible and other religious texts. Discovering Jordan Peterson's lecture series, Maps of Meaning: The Architecture of Belief and Personality and Its Transformations, I was struck by the interconnectedness of religion, philosophy, science, and mathematics. These lectures facilitated a deeper self-exploration and understanding, encouraging me to embark on a more challenging yet rewarding path.

Tao

Over the years, as I've explored the people I look up to and tried to emulate their paths and philosophies, I've realized it's time to forge my own path. No one can be a complete mentor to me except myself. I'm not the protagonist in anyone else's story, but I am in mine. To navigate life, I need to pay attention to the natural order and the Tao of the world. Rather than forcing myself onto a path I think will lead to success, I can adopt the philosophy of wuwei from Taoism, following the natural law and finding my own meaning in life.
